## 1. The honest summary first

You picked a sensible combination. Wholesale-and-resell with a £500–£2,000 budget,
working full-time, selling on eBay and TikTok Shop, is a **real, legal, beatable
business** — not a get-rich-quick scheme. Here is the blunt version before the detail:

- **eBay is where you make your first reliable money.** Lower risk, instant UK
  buyer base, you only pay fees when you sell. Start here to build cash flow.
- **TikTok Shop is where the *growth* is, but it eats content and ad budget.**
  Industry "minimum viable launch" figures float around £3,500+ once you add
  content production and ads. On a £500–£2,000 budget you do TikTok the *lean*
  way: your own phone, organic videos, and affiliate creators paid on commission
  (not upfront). Treat it as channel #2 once eBay is paying the bills.
- **The £500–£2,000 budget is for STOCK, not tools.** Spend almost nothing on
  software at the start. Free/cheap tools plus your own labour is the right call.
- **Margins are thin.** After eBay/TikTok fees (~13–15%), VAT considerations,
  shipping, and packaging, you need products you can buy at roughly **a third of
  what you sell them for** to make this work. Buy-at-£4-sell-at-£12 is the kind
  of maths you are hunting for.

## 5. Invoices & eBay authenticity (read this carefully)

This was your specific question — here's the real picture:

- **Why invoices matter:** eBay can ask a seller to prove stock is genuine and
  legally sourced, especially for branded goods. A proper invoice from a
  VAT-registered UK wholesaler is your evidence. AliExpress screenshots or random
  dropship links generally are **not** acceptable.
- **What a valid invoice needs:** supplier's name + address + VAT number, your
  business details, date, itemised products, quantities, prices, and VAT shown.
  All the real wholesalers in §4 provide these automatically.
- **eBay "Authenticity Guarantee"** is a *separate* programme — it routes certain
  high-value items (watches £1,500+, sneakers, handbags, fashion, jewellery,
  trading cards) through a third-party authenticator before the buyer gets them.
  You can ignore this at the start unless you sell those categories.
- **The invoice you give *buyers*** (VAT invoices on your sales) is different from
  the supplier invoices you *receive*. Once VAT-registered you'll issue VAT
  invoices; tools exist to auto-generate HMRC-compliant ones from eBay orders.

**Bottom line:** Buy from the §4 suppliers, keep every invoice in an organised
folder (digital + backup), and you're covered for both eBay's checks and HMRC.

### "Is buying from a wholesaler enough to list — or do I need brand approval?"

This is the question that trips up every beginner, because **eBay and Amazon work
in opposite ways.**

**eBay = open door, checked only if there's a problem.**
- There is **no upfront brand-approval step.** Open a business account, create a
  listing, it goes live the same day. Nobody pre-approves you.
- Your wholesaler invoice is a **safety net, not an entry ticket** — you only
  produce it *if* eBay challenges you (a buyer claims a fake, or a brand reports
  the listing). Then you show it and you're fine.
- The real risk is **VeRO** (Verified Rights Owner): some brands register with
  eBay so they can have listings of their products removed — even genuine ones.
  Repeat strikes can suspend the account. So on eBay the danger isn't "getting
  approved," it's "picking a protected brand." **Check eBay's VeRO list before
  listing any branded item.**

**Amazon = locked door, approval required first.**
- Many brands/categories (beauty, grocery, Nike, Samsung, Bosch, etc.) are
  **"gated."** You cannot list until Amazon approves you — called **"ungating."**
- Ungating needs **commercial invoices from an *authorised* distributor**,
  typically **10–100 units**, dated within 90–180 days, business name matching
  exactly. Retail receipts don't count. This is why we **start on eBay, not Amazon.**

**What this means in practice:**
1. **Sell non-branded / generic products first** (the gadgets & generic beauty
   accessories in §3). No brand to approve, **zero VeRO risk** — the wholesaler
   invoice covers everything. Cleanest possible start.
2. **Branded clearance** (pallet suppliers): goods are genuine and UK law generally
   lets you resell legitimately-bought branded items — but **check the VeRO list
   for that brand first**; if it's on there, expect hassle and consider something else.
3. **Keep every supplier invoice** regardless — your defence on eBay, and your
   ungating ticket if you later expand to Amazon.

So for this plan (eBay + generic/clearance stock), **buying from the §4 suppliers
IS enough to start selling.** The approval gauntlet only appears on Amazon or with
protected designer brands.

## 6. The real cost of selling (fees breakdown 2026)

Build these into every pricing decision. This is where new sellers lose money.

### eBay UK (business account)
- **Final Value Fee:** ~6.9%–14.9% by category (most items **9.9%–12.9%**).
- **Per-order fee:** £0.30 (orders ≤£10) or £0.40 (orders >£10).
- **Regulatory Operating Fee:** 0.35%.
- **VAT:** 20% on the fees (reclaimable *only* once VAT-registered).
- **Rule of thumb:** assume **~13–15% of sale price** goes to eBay all-in.
- Note: private sellers sell fee-free since Oct 2024, but a real business **must**
  use a **business account** (legal requirement when buying to resell).

### TikTok Shop UK
- **Platform commission:** flat **9%** most categories (some Electronics/Beauty 5%),
  on net GMV (after seller discounts), as of Jan 2026.
- **Self-ship fee:** £0.50 per order if you post it yourself.
- **Smart Promotions:** optional auto-ads taking up to **3.5% of GMV** (4.5% in
  campaigns) — **often ON by default, so check and disable it** until you choose
  to advertise.
- **Affiliate commission:** whatever % you offer creators (you set it; only paid
  when they sell — great low-risk way to get content).

### Worked example (a £20 gadget)
```
Sell price (TikTok Shop):           £20.00
Cost from wholesaler (~1/3):        -£6.50
TikTok 9% commission:               -£1.80
Self-ship fee:                      -£0.50
Postage (Royal Mail small parcel):  -£3.29
Packaging:                          -£0.40
---------------------------------------------
Gross profit per unit:              ≈ £7.51  (≈ 37% margin)
```
That's a *healthy* margin — but only because the buy price is ~1/3 of sale price.
If you'd paid £10 for that item, you'd make ~£4 and one return wipes out three sales.

6. Pick **3–5 test products** in Home/Kitchen gadgets or beauty accessories,
   £4–£8 buy price, that demo well on video.
7. Place **small first orders** (low MOQ suppliers from §4). Don't go big yet.
8. While stock ships, photograph competitors' listings for pricing, and I'll
   draft your eBay titles + descriptions + TikTok scripts.

### Week 3 — List & launch (spend: packaging ~£40)
9. List all products on **eBay** first (optimised titles, good photos, competitive
   price to win early sales + feedback).
10. List on **TikTok Shop**, turn **OFF Smart Promotions** for now.
11. Film **3–5 short videos per product** on a phone — the "satisfying demo" format.
    Post daily. Organic reach on TikTok doesn't need followers.

15. Reinvest profit into more stock of proven winners — *not* new untested products.

## 8. Tax & legal (don't skip — HMRC now watches platforms)

- **From 2026, eBay and TikTok report seller sales to HMRC monthly.** "Casual
  selling" is no longer invisible. Register and declare from day one.
- **Sole trader** to start: register with HMRC, file a Self Assessment, pay tax on
  profit. Free and simple.
- **VAT:** You only *must* register once turnover hits **£90,000** in a rolling
  12 months. Below that it's optional. (Voluntary registration lets you reclaim
  VAT on stock/fees but means charging VAT — usually not worth it at the start.)
- **Keep records:** every supplier invoice, every sale, every expense. A shoebox
  won't cut it now that platforms report automatically.
- **Business insurance** & **distance-selling/consumer rights:** UK online sellers
  must offer 14-day returns under Consumer Contracts Regulations. Build a clear
  returns policy.

### ⚠️ Visa note (important for whoever is "the face")
- **Student visa = CANNOT be self-employed or run a business.** No sole trader,
  no company directorship/stake, no trading. UKVI prohibits it — breaching it
  risks the future Graduate/PSW visa.
- **Graduate (PSW) visa = self-employment and running a business IS allowed.**
  Wait until PSW is granted before putting your name on the business.
- **A partner/family member with UK citizenship, settled status, or full right
  to work** can be the sole trader / director **now**, with no restriction.
- Until PSW lands, the business should legally belong to the person with the
  right to trade. **Confirm with an OISC immigration adviser before acting.**

### Ltd or sole trader?
- **No, an Ltd is not required.** Start as a **sole trader** (simplest, cheapest).
- **Switch to Ltd later** once profit is meaningful (~£30k+/yr) for tax efficiency
  and limited liability. Needs an accountant.

### Tax at a glance
- **Sole trader:** tax on profit via Self Assessment — £0–£12,570 free, then 20% /
  40% (>£50,270) / 45% (>£125k), plus Class 4 NIC (~6%/2%).
- **Ltd:** Corporation Tax 19–25% on profit; take salary + dividends (dividend tax
  8.75%/33.75%, first £500 free).
- Both: VAT only at £90k turnover; platforms report sales to HMRC from 2026.

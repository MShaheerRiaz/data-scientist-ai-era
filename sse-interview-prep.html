History & Background

SSE plc was formed in 1998 through the merger of Scottish Hydro-Electric and Southern Electric. The company is listed on the London Stock Exchange and is a constituent of the FTSE 100 index, making it one of the UK's largest listed companies by market capitalisation.

Headquartered in Perth, Scotland, SSE has transformed from a regional electricity supplier into one of Europe's leading low-carbon energy companies. It operates across the UK and Ireland, with a growing international renewables presence.

SSE's strategic focus since 2018 has been a full pivot to low-carbon electricity infrastructure — it sold its household energy supply business to focus entirely on networks, renewables, and flexible generation. This makes it uniquely positioned as a pure-play energy transition business.

Core Mission

SSE's mission centres on enabling the UK's transition to net zero by 2050. Its five-year £33bn investment plan (announced November 2025) is one of the largest single private infrastructure investment programmes in the UK — 80% directed at electricity grid infrastructure.

Interview Prep

Ace the Interview — From Real Candidates

Tips sourced from TheStudentRoom, AptitudePrep, and SSE's own recruiter guidance

The Full Recruitment Process

1
Online Application
CV submission + motivational questions about why SSE and your chosen division. About 200 graduates hired per year — competitive but achievable.
Tailor your motivation to the specific business unit (Transmission, Renewables, Distribution, Thermal). Generic answers get screened out.
2
Online Tests (SHL) — The Hardest Stage for Many
Part A: Situational Judgement Test (SJT) — 16 questions. Work-based scenarios testing alignment with SSE's values. Answer honestly; don't game it.

Part B: General Ability Test (SHL) — 24 questions of numerical, analytical and deductive reasoning. Described by candidates as "absolutely brutal / time-pressured — timed out at Q8."

No verbal reasoning component for engineering roles.
Practice at shl.com — use their exact test format. Focus on speed as much as accuracy. The SHL test is the same format used by NatWest, KPMG etc.
3
Video Interview
4 questions. 3 minutes to think, 4 minutes to answer each. Can be pre-recorded or live.

Real questions asked (from TheStudentRoom candidates):
  • "Why SSE Thermal / [your chosen division]? What do you seek to obtain from the role?"
  • "Name 3 energy challenges currently facing SSE [your division]"
  • Competency: a time you demonstrated safety
  • Competency: a time you showed initiative
Ensure good lighting, professional background, test your audio. Candidates who passed say: be yourself, communicate clearly, and reference the specific division.
4
Assessment Centre
Virtual or in-person. Includes: group discussion, individual presentation (brief is intentionally vague — structure it yourself), role-play scenario, possibly a final technical interview.

Candidates noted the Transmission interview was "more technical than expected." The presentation brief gives minimal direction — prepare to make your own structure with an intro, 3 key points, and a recommendation.
Ask the recruiter in advance: "Can you tell me the format of the assessment centre?" — SSE recruiters encourage this and can share example questions or grant extra time for reasonable adjustments.
⚠ Visa Sponsorship: SSE graduate engineering roles currently fall below the Skilled Worker minimum salary threshold. No visa sponsorship is available for graduate engineering positions. Confirm your right to work in the UK before applying.

The STAR Technique — SSE's Official Method

Structure every competency answer using STAR

S Situation
Set the scene — what was the context, when, where?
T Task
What was your specific responsibility or challenge?
A Action
What did YOU specifically do? (Use "I", not "we")
R Result
What was the outcome? Quantify if possible.
EE Masters example: "During my Masters dissertation [S], I was tasked with modelling the transient stability of a grid-connected wind farm under fault conditions [T]. I implemented a dynamic simulation in MATLAB/Simulink, validated against published IEEE test cases, and proposed a modified control strategy for the turbine converter [A]. This reduced the settling time after a fault by 23% and formed the basis of a paper submitted to IEEE PES [R]." — then link it to SSE's grid stability work at SSEN Transmission.

Common Interview Questions — Model Answer Structures

What SSE is looking for: Genuine knowledge of the company and specific division, aligned values, long-term ambition, not generic "I like renewables."

Structure your answer around:

  • SSE's unique position as a pure-play energy transition business (not a legacy generalist)
  • Specific project relevant to your division (Dogger Bank for Renewables, Pathway to 2030 for Transmission, Peterhead for Thermal)
  • The scale: £33bn plan, £7M/day — you want to work somewhere making a real impact, not incremental change
  • How your EE Masters skills map directly to SSE's engineering needs
  • SSE's progression: professional chartership (IET/IMechE), 200+ graduates, structured development
Avoid: "I'm passionate about renewable energy" with no specifics. That applies to 1,000 other candidates. Name the project. Name the technology.

This question was reported by real candidates in video interviews for SSE Thermal and other divisions. Prepare your three for your specific division.

For SSE Thermal / Flexible Generation:

  • Grid intermittency: Renewables can't always meet demand — flexible dispatchable generation is essential but must decarbonise
  • Carbon capture economics: CCS is technically proven but financing a first-of-kind commercial CCS plant (Peterhead) at scale is complex
  • Hydrogen transition: Converting gas infrastructure to hydrogen-ready by the late 2020s requires new engineering standards

For SSEN Transmission / Distribution:

  • Grid constraint: North–south transmission bottlenecks limit how much Scottish renewable power reaches English demand centres
  • Supply chain capacity: Substation equipment (transformers, switchgear) has 2–3 year lead times — managing procurement at scale
  • Planning permission: New OHL and substation infrastructure faces community opposition, delaying projects

Why it matters: Safety is SSE's number one value. A weak answer here is a red flag regardless of technical brilliance. This is the most important competency question.

STAR structure ideas for EE candidates:

  • HV laboratory work — following lock-out/tag-out procedures, conducting a pre-task risk assessment before energising equipment
  • Identifying a hazard during a project (e.g. insufficient insulation clearance in a design) and stopping work to escalate
  • Industrial placement or summer job where you observed or contributed to a safety briefing / tool-box talk
  • Research or lab work where you designed the safety case for operating at elevated voltage or current
Close your answer by connecting it to SSE: "This experience reinforced for me that in an environment like SSE's — where engineers work on 400kV substations and offshore platforms — a safety-first culture isn't just important, it's what prevents serious harm."

What they're testing: Analytical thinking, engineering judgement, structured problem-solving, and your ability to explain technical concepts clearly to a mixed audience.

Ideal sources from EE Masters study:

  • Final-year dissertation / research project — power system simulation, converter design, protection study
  • A challenging coursework where your initial approach failed and you adapted
  • A group design challenge with competing constraints (cost vs. performance vs. reliability)
  • Any real-world placement or project involving measurement, analysis, and a recommended solution

Tips: Don't over-explain the technical depth to the point the interviewer is lost. Explain the problem in plain language first, then the method, then the result. Show your thinking process — this is what differentiates a Masters graduate.

What they're testing: Communication, collaboration across disciplines, conflict resolution, your role within a team (not just as a leader but as a reliable contributor).

  • Group design project at university — describe your specific role and how you handled disagreements on technical approach
  • Mention interdisciplinary aspects if relevant (working with software or mechanical engineers)
  • If you had to explain your EE work to non-specialists in the team, highlight that — SSE teams are multidisciplinary
SSE explicitly looks for candidates who are "flexible and agile" and can "address knowledge gaps" — demonstrating you adapted your communication style for different team members scores highly.

What they're testing: Genuine interest in the industry, strategic awareness, ability to connect macro trends to engineering implications.

Strong answer elements:

  • Offshore wind dominance: SSE targets 16GW by 2032; UK government target is 50GW by 2030 — offshore will be the backbone of UK generation
  • Grid transformation: The transmission and distribution networks will require more investment than the generation fleet — exactly where SSE is directing 80% of its £33bn
  • Hydrogen economy: Green hydrogen and CCS will decarbonise industrial processes and provide seasonal storage — SSE Thermal is positioning for this
  • Digitalisation & AI: Smart grid, real-time network optimisation, predictive maintenance will change how network engineers work
  • Energy storage: Battery storage at grid scale will fundamentally change how the system balances supply and demand without gas peakers
Anchor your answer to SSE: "SSE's £33bn investment plan is essentially a bet on that infrastructure future — and I want to be one of the engineers building it."

SSE recruiters specifically recommend asking this question — it shows you're evaluating them as much as they're evaluating you:

  • "If I were successful, what would you expect from me within the first 90 days?" — recommended directly by SSE's own recruiter blog
  • "Which of the Pathway to 2030 projects would a graduate in this team have exposure to earliest?"
  • "How is the team structured between office and site-based work in this role?"
  • "What does the path to IET chartership look like from this graduate programme?"
  • "How is SSE thinking about the skills pipeline for the RIIO-T3 period and the scale-up beyond 2030?"
Avoid asking about salary, holidays, or anything that's in the job description. Ask questions that prove you've done deeper research than the job advert.

Red Flags to Avoid

  • Not knowing which SSE business unit you applied for — this is a basic disqualifier in video interviews
  • Generic "I like renewable energy" answers — without naming specific projects, technologies, or SSE investment plans
  • Confusing SSE plc with Stockholm School of Economics — a real pitfall if you Google "SSE" without context
  • Bluffing on technical questions — SSE values honesty. Say "I haven't worked on that specifically, but I'd approach it by..." rather than blagging
  • Not preparing STAR examples in advance — the video interview gives only 3 minutes to think, which is not enough to improvise a strong structured answer
  • Overlooking safety — candidates who don't demonstrate a genuine safety mindset are unlikely to progress in an HV engineering environment
